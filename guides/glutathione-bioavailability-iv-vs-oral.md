# Glutathione Bioavailability: IV vs Oral Routes Reference

Reference document on glutathione (GSH) absorption and systemic distribution across administration routes. Compiled from peer-reviewed pharmacokinetic literature for research and clinical reference.

## Why bioavailability matters

Glutathione is a tripeptide (γ-glutamyl-cysteinyl-glycine) with strong intracellular antioxidant activity. Its therapeutic effect depends on reaching the intended tissue compartments in active reduced form (GSH), not the oxidized form (GSSG). Different routes produce dramatically different systemic exposure profiles.

## Oral glutathione: first-pass barrier

Oral glutathione faces enzymatic hydrolysis by intestinal γ-glutamyltransferase before absorption. Conventional GSH supplementation studies show negligible elevation in plasma reduced glutathione even at 1000 mg doses. Liposomal and sublingual formulations partially bypass this hydrolysis but still achieve only fractional bioavailability compared to parenteral routes.

| Form | Approximate bioavailability | Notes |
|------|------------------------------|-------|
| Standard oral GSH | <1% (unchanged) | Hydrolyzed in upper GI tract |
| Liposomal oral GSH | 5-10% (reported) | Manufacturer claims; independent data sparse |
| Sublingual GSH | 5-15% | Mucosal absorption, partial enzyme bypass |
| Sublingual S-acetyl-GSH | 15-25% | Acetylation protects against hydrolysis |

## IV/IM glutathione: parenteral pharmacokinetics

Intravenous administration bypasses the gut entirely. Plasma GSH peaks within minutes of infusion and declines with a short distribution half-life (10-15 minutes) followed by a slower elimination phase. Tissue uptake into erythrocytes, hepatocytes, and other compartments occurs during the peak window.

Intramuscular administration produces a flatter, more sustained plasma profile due to slower release from the injection site. Reported half-lives in muscle depot are 30-60 minutes.

## Systemic exposure comparison

For a standard 600 mg dose:
- **Oral standard**: peak plasma GSH increase typically <2 µmol/L above baseline
- **Liposomal oral**: peak increase 5-15 µmol/L
- **IM**: peak increase 30-50 µmol/L sustained over 1-2 hours
- **IV push**: peak increase 200-400 µmol/L for 5-10 minutes

## Implications for protocol selection

Selection of route should follow the clinical or research target:
- **Hepatocyte and oxidative stress modulation**: IV or IM, frequent dosing
- **Skin and cosmetic applications**: IV protocols with adjunct vitamin C (Tationil-style protocols)
- **Long-term supplementation in healthy populations**: Liposomal or sublingual oral, for adherence
- **Acute glutathione depletion (e.g., research models)**: IV
